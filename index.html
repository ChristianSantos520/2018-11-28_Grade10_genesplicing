Gene Splicing

by Christian Santos

What is Gene Splicing?

Gene splicing is when certain sections of genes or genomes are inserted into a different genome, which is in a different living organism. Gene splicing happens before mRNA translation in eukaryotic cells and is part of genetic engineering. In the DNA, the enzymes will act as the cutting tool that removes a section of the DNA. Later, a new part of DNA, that was removed from another gene, will attach to fill the empty spot, completing the DNA. Another kind of enzyme, which is called ligase, will act as the gluing tool so the DNA will not fall apart. The purpose of this process is to change the DNA code so that when the DNA is read, the function of what the DNA is used for will change. For example, there might be a defect in the DNA and the process of gene splicing will fix this defect, possibly improving the health of the organism if it is successful.

How is Gene Splicing Helping the Advancement of Genetic Research?

Every week a Canadian dies from cystic fibrosis, which mainly affects the lungs and immune system. Using genetic splicing, scientists can conduct research on cystic fibrosis, since gene splicing naturally occurs and cystic fibrosis is caused when an error happens in the gene splicing. Many other kinds of disorders come from splicing. Scientists know what happens normally in gene splicing and their idea is to focus on what can go wrong in gene splicing. Therefore, this research will allow them to gain knowledge for many other diseases.

Problems and Difficulties

If gene splicing is not done right, it will lead to mutations that could lead to disease. When the error happens, it is very difficult to fix. Scientists may need to change or fix 40 to 50 amino acids, which makes up one protein. Another problem with gene splicing is that theologians are concerned about the ethics of gene splicing, by allowing scientists to design organisms the way they want. For example, if parents want their child to be taller, they could ask scientists to alter their child’s genes. The result would be an alteration in the increase of height. Theologians say that gene splicing is like “playing God”. The Synagogue Council of America, the United States Catholic Conference, and the National Council of Churches are especially concerned about this because scientists are changing the meaning of the “image of God”. Then God said, “Let us make mankind in our image, in our likeness, so that they may rule over the fish in the sea and the birds in the sky, over the livestock and all the wild animals. and over all the creatures that move along the ground.” Genesis 1:26 (NIV Version).

Latest Project and Discoveries

Using gene splicing, by removing unnecessary sections in the DNA and replacing them with better ones, can help an organism. There is a type of gene splicing called CRISPR, which makes the process cheap and quick. CRISPR (Clusters of Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats) takes a few days to complete, and only costs thirty dollars. This gene editing process uses some bacteria that can defend themselves from viruses by having a gene-editing mechanism. These kinds of bacteria use an enzyme called Cas9 to use as scissors. These bacteria cut out certain genes from viruses and stores them in their own immune system. CRISPR can provide a cure for sickle cell anemia and muscular dystrophy. This advancement in technology is already in use for making crops invulnerable to harmful fungi and making mosquitos not pass down malaria to the next generation of mosquitos 99.5 percent of the time. However, the most successful use of CRISPR is the process of curing heredity diseases. Therefore, people should stop wishing to use gene splicing to create every organism they want, but use gene splicing to help cure diseases.
